Sojourn-Vacation Planning App

THE PROBLEM: Travelers with families need a way to streamline all of the research involved with planning a trip and still find what they want.

THE SOLUTION: Sojourn is the voice activated trip planner that finds everything you want, within your budget, so you don’t waste time.

Role: UX Designer

Tools: Figma, Miro, Invision

User Research

Research Objectives: Discover what the hardest part of travel planning is and what would make it easier.

Method: Conducted 5 interviews with 18-34 year olds who have at least one child (statistically this is the group that travels the most). Surveyed 10 participants who had taken at least one trip in the past year.

We found that most of our interviewees needed to spend a lot of time planning but disliked how time consuming it was, however they didn’t consider using a travel agent because they wanted to do things themselves.

"I just like to do everything on my own"-Ashley
"We needed a lot more planning, it would have avoided a lot of arguing” -Fern
“I expect it to be a long process...in a way I dread it”. -Tiffany

Survey Data

Our survey revealed that most of our participants traveled with their families and that 80% spent over a day planning vacations. 50% spent anywhere from 4 days to 4 weeks planning!

Affinity Mapping

After grouping together responses from both the survey and interviews we noticed that controlling the budget was also an important step for our vacation planners

Definition & Ideation

Problem Statement: Travelers with families have the problem of spending a large amount of time finding deals and well rated flights, lodging, and activities when planning a vacation. Our solution should streamline the vacation planning process while providing enough options so they feel like they are getting what they want.

After a brainstorming sessions to generate ideas on features the app should have, I prioritized some of the more popular ones

Value Proposition Statement

Sojourn. Vacation planning at the tip of your tongue.

The voice activated trip planner that finds everything you want within your budget so you don’t waste time.

Storyboard

Samantha wants to take a summer vacation with her family. She needs to stay on budget & wants everyone to have fun but dreads spending hours clicking through a million web pages to plan the details.

Her boyfriend tells her to try the Sojourn app to help her get want she want done quickly.

She downloads the app & tells it what she’s trying to plan.

Sojourn notifies her & shows her options based on her criteria within an hour.

She reads reviews & decides on what she likes & books trip.

Samantha is happy she gets to spend more time being excited about details & is done planning.

Testing & Iterating

Objective: To discover if travelers would use a single app to plan multiple parts of their trip. I want to know if they able to locate the search results after entering their details and if they would choose to set profile preferences.

Method: Test on 4 users remotely

    Tasks:
  1. Navigate through the trip planning portion of the app without skipping any questions.
  2. Locate menu with options for search results
  3. Decide to set all of the profile preference options

Feedback:

Participants did not skip through any trip planning questions but had trouble getting to this section after signing up

Participants had to look and click around for search results which was not ideal

Participants began to complete task 3 without me asking in the 2nd task because the button to “Complete Profile” was at the bottom of the screen for task 2

Final Thoughts

I hit a roadblock after completing the initial research. I knew that users didn’t like the amount of time they spent planning trips but I didn’t really understand why. Some of the sites they were using offered ways to make the process easier. I had to call interviews back to dig deeper. My interview questions could have been more probing.

There were a lot of things I learned after user testing. I assumed that if actionable options were in writing, users would read them and understand the step but just because something is written out doesn’t mean that users will know or want to read it to move to the next step. UX design is really about organizing things in a way so that users don’t need to stop and read steps, but rather they can move through a process in a way that feels natural.

Although I like the final concept of a voice activated app, I’m curious how well it would work without being able to test it’s true functionality. For example, how background noise or different accents would affect its ability to understand the user or if they would really prefer to speak their choices. I would love to be able to test out a more hi-fidelity prototype. This was fun!
